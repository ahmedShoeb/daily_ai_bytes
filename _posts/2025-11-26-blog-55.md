## Key Points

- Google DeepMind has hired Aaron Saunders, former CTO of Boston Dynamics, as VP of hardware engineering.
- DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis plans for Gemini to function as a robot operating system, akin to Android, compatible with various robot types.
- Boston Dynamics is renowned for its legged and humanoid robots, with Saunders having been a key figure in their development.
- Google DeepMind is intensifying its focus on developing AI models to control robotic hardware systems.
- Hassabis anticipates a significant "breakthrough moment" for AI-powered robotics in the near future.
- The article notes the growing competition in robotics, with US startups and Chinese companies like Unitree making advancements.
- Hassabis emphasizes his interest in the AI "brain part" of robotics, highlighting Gemini's multimodal capabilities for this purpose.

## Key Topics Discussed

Hey podcast listeners! We've got some really interesting news from the world of AI and robotics. Google DeepMind, a major player in artificial intelligence, has just brought on board Aaron Saunders, the former chief technology officer from Boston Dynamics. If you know Boston Dynamics, you know they're the folks behind those incredible back-flipping and dancing robots, so this is a big deal! Saunders is joining DeepMind as the VP of hardware engineering, and it signals a significant push into robotics for Google. DeepMind's CEO, Demis Hassabis, has a clear vision for their flagship AI model, Gemini. He wants Gemini to essentially become the 'Android of robotics,' a powerful operating system that can work across all sorts of robot designs, whether they're humanoids or other configurations. This is a pretty ambitious goal, aiming to provide a foundational AI system that can be adopted by various robot manufacturers. The article highlights Boston Dynamics' impressive work with legged robots and humanoids, areas where Saunders has played a crucial role. Google DeepMind has actually been involved in robotics research for years, but with the increasing interest in advanced robotic forms like humanoids, they're really doubling down on developing AI models specifically for controlling robotic hardware. Hassabis is very optimistic, predicting that AI-powered robotics is on the cusp of a major breakthrough in the next couple of years. It's also interesting to note the broader landscape. Boston Dynamics is now majority-owned by Hyundai, having previously been part of Alphabet. We're seeing more and more players entering the field, with US startups like Agility Robotics, Figure AI, 1x, and even Tesla, with Elon Musk's ambitious plans for Optimus humanoids, making strides. Not to be outdone, Chinese companies such as Unitree are also advancing rapidly, offering more affordable legged machines and even surpassing Boston Dynamics in some areas for industrial supply. However, Hassabis's primary focus remains firmly on the software side—the 'AI brain part' of it all—especially leveraging Gemini's multimodal capabilities for robotics. It sounds like the future of robotics is going to be incredibly dynamic!
