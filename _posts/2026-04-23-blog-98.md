## Key Points

- Researchers discovered that even so-called 'uncensored' language models still subtly avoid using certain charged words through a phenomenon called 'the flinch'
- The flinch is a gap between what a word deserves based on pure fluency and what probability a model actually assigns it, happening without any refusal messages or warnings
- Researchers measured flinch across 1,117 charged words in 6 categories: Anti-China, Anti-America, Anti-Europe, Slurs, Sexual terms, and Violence-related words
- The study analyzed 7 pretrained models from 5 different labs including EleutherAI's Pythia, Allen AI's OLMo, Alibaba's Qwen, Google's Gemma models, and OpenAI's GPT-OSS-20B
- They found that even refusal-ablated 'uncensored' models (like heretic-v2-9b) actually show increased flinch compared to their base pretrained versions
- Commercial pretrained models show more flinch than open-data models, with Pythia (unfiltered 2020 data) showing the least flinch and serving as the baseline
- Gemma-2-9b showed particularly high flinch on slurs (93/100), but newer Gemma-4-31b showed much lower flinch scores across categories
- The flinch shape varies significantly between labs and model versions, revealing different censorship priorities in training data filtering
