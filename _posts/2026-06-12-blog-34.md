## Key Points

- The article argues that text optimization (prompts, context, memory, retrieval systems) should be taken more seriously as a legitimate learning mechanism alongside weight optimization in AI systems.
- Text optimization is more sample-efficient than weight optimization in low-data scenarios, acting as a compact 'staging ground' for information before it's potentially distilled into weights.
- It enables 'update-time compute' scaling, allowing systems to spend more compute learning from single experiences through reflective hypothesis testing.
- While weights provide amortization benefits for stable information, text optimization is better for volatile, local, or not-yet-trusted information that shouldn't be permanently baked into weights.
- The author argues that the research community has overcorrected toward treating weights as the only 'real learning', when external text artifacts play a similar functional role to scientific theories in human cognition.
- Empirical evidence shows significant headroom for improving systems through text optimization alone, with fixed-model behavior improving through better context and execution environments.
- The author calls for more rigorous research on text optimization, including theoretical analysis, better evaluation frameworks, and understanding the design space for text-layer architectures.
- Text optimization faces challenges from bad science due to low barriers to entry, but this should motivate more rigorous study given its practical importance in deployed AI systems.
- External text artifacts are compared to cognitive prosthetics in human intelligence, similar to how humans use written text and external memory systems to amplify their capabilities.
