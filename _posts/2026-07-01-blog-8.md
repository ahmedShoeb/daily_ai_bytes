## Key Points

- Reward models are widely used in reinforcement learning but their role is poorly understood
- Unlike verifiable rewards that produce binary scores, reward models produce continuous scores that can be sensitive to fine-grained differences
- Research shows this apparent strength is actually a weakness: many popular reward models are oversensitive, assigning different scores to equally good responses
- Theoretically, seemingly perfect reward models can be highly oversensitive
- Empirically, oversensitivity can lead to bad policies
- Researchers propose evaluating reward models using distinct measures of 'discriminative ability' and 'specificity' (complement of oversensitivity)
- They introduce a training-free algorithm using Monte Carlo dropout on neural reward models to produce discrete reward clusters
- Theoretically, discretizations exist that reduce oversensitivity with minimal loss of discriminative ability
- Empirically, discretizing rewards leads to less reward hacking and better policies in both controlled and natural RL settings
