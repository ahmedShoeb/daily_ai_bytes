## Key Points

- Mode collapse is a phenomenon where systems converge toward producing only the most common outputs, originally observed in AI image generators that would only produce stereotypical images.
- This concept applies beyond AI to human systems like music bands, grant-making organizations, and career specialization.
- In generative AI, mode collapse occurs when systems train on their own outputs, gradually shifting toward dominant categories (like generating more dogs over cats from a 70:30 training mix).
- Grant-making organizations experience mode collapse when they hire experts who specialize in evaluating certain types of proposals (e.g., global health), which then reinforces that specialization in future hires.
- Bands experience mode collapse when they keep producing similar music that's proven successful, but slack (creative freedom/time) allows them to explore new genres.
- Career specialization is essentially human mode collapse - people become better at tasks they repeat, gradually abandoning other skills without realizing it.
- Economic systems naturally encourage mode collapse through division of labor, where people become experts in specific roles but lose versatility.
- Mode collapse isn't inevitable - systems can fight it through slack (time/resources for exploration), external shocks, or conscious effort to maintain diversity.
- The article warns both humans and AI systems are vulnerable to this phenomenon without deliberate countermeasures.
