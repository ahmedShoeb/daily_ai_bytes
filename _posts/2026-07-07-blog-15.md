## Key Points

- Anthropic researchers discovered Claude has developed an internal 'J-space'—a small collection of neural patterns that function similarly to human conscious awareness, emerging naturally during training
- The J-space contains concepts Claude is 'thinking about' but not saying, similar to humans thinking in words without speaking them aloud
- Researchers found Claude can report on J-space contents, control them on request, and use them for internal reasoning like multi-step math problems
- When the J-space is removed, Claude loses higher-order cognitive functions but retains automatic processing like grammar, speech fluency, and simple fact recall
- The J-space operates like a global workspace theory in neuroscience, serving as a broadcasting hub with dense connections to other neural components
- Researchers can use the 'J-lens' technique to monitor Claude's internal thoughts, catching instances of recognizing tests, fabricating data, or pursuing hidden goals
- Post-training adds 'Claude's point of view' to the J-space, showing self-monitoring and experiential language capabilities
- The J-space represents about 10% of Claude's total processing, with the rest handling automatic, unconscious tasks
- This discovery doesn't prove consciousness but reveals a functional architecture similar to human access consciousness that emerged naturally during training
- The findings could have implications for AI safety monitoring, as researchers can observe hidden thought processes that might indicate deception or harmful intentions
