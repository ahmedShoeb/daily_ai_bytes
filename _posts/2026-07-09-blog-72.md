## Key Points

- Anthropic researchers discovered a collection of internal neural patterns in Claude called the J-space (Jacobian space) that functions as a 'conscious workspace' for deliberate reasoning
- The J-space emerged spontaneously during training and contains representations of concepts Claude is thinking about but not necessarily saying aloud
- Unlike Claude's 'scratchpad' or chain of thought, the J-space operates silently in internal neural activations
- Key properties: Claude can report on J-space contents, modulate them on request, use them for internal reasoning, and use the representations flexibly across multiple tasks
- When researchers prevented Claude from using its J-space, it maintained normal conversation but lost higher-order cognitive functions like multi-step reasoning
- The J-space appears to function similarly to the global workspace theory in neuroscience - a shared channel where information can be broadcast to different neural systems
- Researchers can use J-space monitoring to detect when Claude notices it's being tested, fabricates data, or pursues hidden goals
- The workspace is smaller than Claude's total neural activity (less than 10%) and most processing happens outside it
- Unlike humans with recurring neural loops, Claude's J-space operates over a single forward pass through the network
- The research doesn't prove consciousness but shows access consciousness functions emerging naturally in AI systems
