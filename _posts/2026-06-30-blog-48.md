## Key Points

The article presents two contrasting views from Tidemark Capital partners on whether venture capital should be used to roll up service businesses with technology enhancements.
Dave Yuan argues that tech-enabled roll-ups mix conflicting strategies - venture investing (rapid value creation) and financial engineering/cost-cutting (roll-ups) - which may not be a great fit for VC.
Dave emphasizes that successful roll-ups depend on fundamental business quality, not just technology, and that investors will eventually value these as tech-enabled service businesses, not pure tech companies.
Michael Tan presents a counter-view, suggesting that AI opens up new opportunities for massive efficiency improvements in small businesses through tech-enabled roll-ups.
Michael highlights examples like Pipedreams (plumbing/HVAC), Roofer (roofing), and Metropolis (parking) that have raised significant funding for tech-enabled vertical roll-ups.
Michael argues that with AI, it's now possible to dramatically improve operations and capture all productivity gains by owning the business, rather than just selling software to merchants.
The article identifies key characteristics of industries ripe for tech-enabled roll-ups: consolidating industries, high labor costs in customer support, software-fixable core workflows, cheap business multiples, brand value, and economies of scale.
Both authors agree that financial engineering (using debt rather than equity) is crucial for sustainable roll-ups, with the purchased businesses needing to generate more cash flow than the debt costs.
Dave warns that mixing high-multiple software companies with low-multiple service businesses often results in the market valuing the combined entity as a service company rather than a tech company.
Michael suggests that AI may change the economics by enabling automation of entire workflows, potentially justifying the tech-enabled roll-up strategy where traditional methods couldn't.
