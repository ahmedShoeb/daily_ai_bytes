## Key Points

The search for an "AlphaFold for materials science" is misguided because materials science is fundamentally more complex than structural biology in almost every way
Proteins have nice properties that materials lack: amino acid sequences largely determine structure, while materials composition doesn't guarantee specific structure due to disorder and complex interfaces
Materials span 8 orders of magnitude in scale (from atomic to centimeter scale) vs. proteins' 4 discrete structural scales, creating far more degrees of freedom
Material properties depend heavily on fabrication techniques, which are path-dependent and noncommutative - unlike proteins made through a single biological process
There's no comprehensive materials database like the Protein Data Bank, and creating one is much harder due to vast property variations and reproducibility issues across labs
Current datasets like the Materials Project rely on Density Functional Theory (DFT) simulations that are often incorrect in important ways
Well-funded AI companies in this space focus on simulation-first approaches despite the severe data bottleneck, while data-focused startups have less funding
The author argues that "data factories" producing standardized, high-throughput experimental data offer the most promising path forward, but this will take decades
Even if a general materials model existed, adoption would be slow due to safety testing requirements, supply chain issues, and the immense challenge of manufacturing scale-up
No one in the materials + AI space has a plausible path to a general materials model, suggesting it will come well after software-only AGI
