## Key Points

- The article challenges the common perception that AI automation is becoming unaffordable as models become more capable
- Analysis of METR's time horizon data shows that cost ratios (AI cost divided by human cost) remain around 3% at frontier models' 50% reliability time horizon
- Cost ratios haven't increased as AI capabilities have improved exponentially over time
- Frontier models' time horizons are doubling every few months while maintaining affordable cost ratios
- Improvements to AI capabilities aren't driven by expensive long tasks - even among successful tasks, cost ratios don't rise with task length
- Even with strict cost caps (as low as 1/32x human cost), progress remains fast with time horizons doubling every ~3 months
- The author disputes Toby Ord's conclusion that AI hourly costs are rising exponentially, arguing Ord's methodology inflates cost estimates
- Increased inference-time spending would shorten, not lengthen, AI timelines relative to METR's predictions
- The first AI systems with human-level intelligence might cost more than human labor, but affordable versions would follow soon after
