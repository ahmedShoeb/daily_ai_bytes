## Key Points

The article discusses major reasons why large pretraining runs often fail, focusing on breaking causality and adding bias
Breaking causality occurs in methods like expert choice routing where token allocation depends on future tokens' preferences, creating information in training not available at inference
Token dropping (ignoring weakly matched tokens) can also break causality, with Gemini 2 Pro mentioned as potentially having this issue
Bias is worse than variance because bias compounds while variance can average out
A key example: early GPT-4 training suffered from an FP16 bug where sums of many small gradients accumulated significant errors above 1024
There's debate whether training failures have a finite set of causes (like diseases in aging) or if new issues will keep emerging at scale
Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) is the default distributed strategy, with each GPU storing only 1/N of parameters and using all-gather before processing each layer
FSDP communication overhead (params × 3) is only 50% more than vanilla data parallelism due to replacing all-reduce with reduce-scatter
FSDP hits scaling limits due to communication bottlenecks and batch size constraints (can't scale beyond number of sequences in batch)
Pipeline parallelism introduces bubbles (idle time at start/end of batch) and slows research iteration due to architectural constraints on layer distribution
