## Key Points

- The Department of War declared Anthropic a 'supply chain risk' after Anthropic refused to allow their AI to be used for mass surveillance and autonomous weapons
- OpenAI signed an agreement with the Department of War shortly after, claiming their models won't be used for mass surveillance or autonomous weapons either
- The core dispute centers on the Department of War's demand for 'all lawful use' of AI systems, which Anthropic worried would permit mass surveillance and autonomous weapons
- Current US laws around mass domestic surveillance have significant loopholes, including legal 'incidental collection' of data on millions of Americans and legal mass analysis of third-party purchased data
- Autonomous weapons are primarily regulated by vague Department of War policies that can be changed at any time, not by binding Congressional law
- The article analyzes OpenAI's FAQ claims about safeguards and finds them legally insufficient and potentially misleading
- Key concerns include: AI could enable bulk analysis of purchased commercial data at scale, removing previous cost/scale barriers to mass surveillance
- Without meaningful safeguards, there's little preventing the government from using AI for purposes like analyzing all citizens' communications to create 'loyalty scores'
- The article raises serious questions about whether AI companies should enable systems that could remove human checks against authoritarian abuses
