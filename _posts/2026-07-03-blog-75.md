## Key Points

- No Free Lunch theorem mathematically proves that no general-purpose optimization algorithm outperforms all others across all possible problems - universal generality is a myth in practical terms
- Finite resources (compute, data, development time) force a trade-off: concentrating resources on specific tasks outperforms distributing them broadly
- Evolutionary biology shows specialists fill niches because performance gains come at costs elsewhere - generalists are competent across many environments but optimal for none
- Competitive markets act as selection mechanisms that eliminate broadly distributed strategies in favor of specialized, high-performing approaches
- Machine learning encounters negative transfer when tasks compete for capacity, causing performance degradation that specialized systems avoid
- Mixture-of-experts architectures achieve breadth through internal specialization rather than uniform generality
- AlphaFold's breakthrough protein structure prediction demonstrates the power of intense domain targeting over general competence
- Scaling (Sutton's Bitter Lesson) addresses domain knowledge (hand-coded features) but doesn't challenge domain specialization (scope decisions)
- The same specialization pattern emerges independently across optimization theory, biology, markets, and machine learning - it's what happens when finite resources meet performance requirements
