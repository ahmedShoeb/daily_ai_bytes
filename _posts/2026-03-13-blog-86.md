## Key Points

- The article analyzes 14 popular AI benchmarks, explaining what each tests, how they were created, and their limitations
- SWE-bench Verified tests ability to fix bugs in 12 popular Python repos, but tasks are limited and likely in training data
- Terminal-Bench 2.0 evaluates using terminals to accomplish Linux tasks, considered relevant for practical coding work
- τ² (Tau-squared) tests customer service scenarios but uses simplified schemas and may not reflect real-world complexity
- MCP Atlas measures ability to use multiple MCP tools, but tasks feel like 'LLM-targeted riddles' rather than practical use
- Computer-Use benchmark tests GUI desktop interactions but has validation issues and lacks controlled environment
- ARC-AGI attempts to test general intelligence through visual puzzles, but has narrow focus on visual patterns
- GPQA Diamond contains difficult science questions but dataset is small (198 questions) and approaching saturation
- MMMU tests multimodal understanding across college subjects but many questions can be answered without images
- GDPVal from OpenAI tests real-world occupational tasks but has conflict of interest and uses outdated single-turn methodology
- FrontierMath was created with OpenAI funding under questionable disclosure practices, raising trust issues
- The author concludes most benchmarks have serious limitations: small datasets, training data contamination, and lack of real-world relevance
- The article advocates for creating custom benchmarks tailored to specific use cases rather than relying on published scores
