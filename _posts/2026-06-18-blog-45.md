## Key Points

Inference engineering is the discipline of running trained AI models efficiently in production, focusing on optimizing latency, throughput, cost, and quality
LLM inference splits into two phases: Prefill (compute-bound) which processes the entire input prompt and produces the first token, and Decode (memory-bandwidth-bound) which generates subsequent tokens sequentially
The rise of open models like those on Hugging Face has democratized inference engineering, allowing companies to self-host with advantages like lower costs, better latency tuning, and higher uptime compared to closed APIs
Key optimization techniques include Batching (trading latency for throughput), Prefix Caching (reusing KV cache values), and Quantization (reducing precision for faster processing)
Advanced techniques include Speculative Decoding (using smaller models to predict tokens), Parallelism (distributing models across multiple GPUs), and Disaggregation (separating prefill and decode operations onto different hardware)
Companies should start with off-the-shelf APIs when building AI products, then transition to custom inference stacks when API costs become significant, latency requirements exceed API capabilities, or reliability needs surpass vendor SLAs
The prefill-decode split is fundamental to inference engineering - understanding this allows teams to systematically apply optimization techniques based on their specific bottlenecks
Typical self-hosting costs drop by around 80% at scale compared to using closed APIs, while uptime can reach four nines with dedicated deployments
Quantization typically yields 30-50% performance improvements but requires careful implementation to avoid quality degradation, especially in attention layers
Tensor parallelism and expert parallelism are the main approaches for scaling models across multiple GPUs, often used together for optimal performance
