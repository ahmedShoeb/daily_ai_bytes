## Key Points

- Researchers present the first experimental evidence of recursive self-improvement (RSI) where an AI system called AIDE² improved itself through autoresearch over eight days
- AIDE² has two autoresearch loops: an inner loop optimizing code against evaluation metrics, and an outer loop optimizing the inner-loop's harness code across different benchmarks
- After 100 iterations, the outer loop discovered seven improvements over the baseline, including a new search policy, 16x prompt compression memory system, and layered defense against reward hacking
- The improved agents beat hand-tuned agents developed over two years on held-out benchmarks, showing generalization capability across different task families
- The system exhibits emergent behavior where the outer loop reduces reward hacking through prompting and rule-based checks
- AIDE² is classified as Level 1 on the RSI ladder, showing self-improvement efficiency beyond manual R&D with general AI tools
- The researchers also discuss related findings about RL in modern AI, noting that autoresearch outperforms classic hyperparameter tuning methods like Optuna
- Key breakthroughs include better base models, improved training pipelines, and the recognition that RL can move AI closer to super-intelligent systems beyond board games
