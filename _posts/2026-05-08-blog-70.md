## Key Points

- The paper addresses the ongoing issue of hallucinations (confident errors) in large language models, even in simple factoid question-answering scenarios.
- Authors argue that most improvements in factuality have come from expanding model knowledge rather than improving boundary awareness (knowing what the model doesn't know).
- They propose that models inherently face a tradeoff between eliminating hallucinations and preserving utility due to limited discriminative power.
- The paper introduces a new framing: treating hallucinations as confident errors delivered without appropriate qualification.
- Authors suggest expressing uncertainty as a third path beyond the answer-or-abstain dichotomy, calling it 'faithful uncertainty'.
- They define faithful uncertainty as aligning linguistic uncertainty with intrinsic uncertainty in the model.
- The paper introduces metacognition as the ability for models to be aware of their own uncertainty and act on it.
- For direct interaction, acting on uncertainty means honestly communicating it to users.
- For agentic systems, metacognition becomes a control layer that governs when to search and what to trust.
- The conclusion emphasizes that metacognition is essential for LLMs to be both trustworthy and capable, and highlights open problems for future research.
