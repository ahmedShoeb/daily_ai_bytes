## Key Points

Tom Tunguz conducted a 5-week experiment using local AI models to see how much daily work could be handled without trillion-parameter cloud models, finding the answer is about 50%.
He categorized 1,478 tasks into 7 categories: Other (35.3%), Scheduling (17.2%), Market Research (13.0%), Summarization (12.4%), Email & Inbound (11.5%), Engineering (9.9%), and Admin (0.7%).
About 50% of tasks can succeed on a local 35B model, with Email & Inbound, Scheduling, Summarization, and Admin tasks totaling 618 tasks (41.8%) being fully local-compatible.
Market Research and Engineering tasks split roughly 50/50 between simple tasks suitable for local models and complex ones requiring cloud models.
The primary reason to use local models is latency - local models on his MacBook Pro M5 process tasks up to 2x faster than cloud models via API.
In a benchmark test, Qwen 3.6 35B on his MacBook versus Claude Opus 4.5 via API showed both completed tasks correctly, but Opus was 20% better on reasoning benchmarks.
Cloud models (like Opus) win on structure and polish with better formatting, while local models (like Qwen) win on brevity, often producing half the tokens.
Localmaxxing (pushing inference to local models) is presented as an inevitable response to tokenmaxxing, becoming more viable as local models improve.
Running local inference extracts compute value from depreciating hardware (like his MacBook Pro) before resale.
