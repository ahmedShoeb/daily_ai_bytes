## Key Points

- The article discusses the growing challenge of creating benchmarks that can effectively upper-bound AI capabilities as AI systems rapidly improve
- Early 2026 marks a worsening situation where many existing benchmarks like METR's Time Horizon suite are being saturated by frontier models
- Creating new benchmarks becomes increasingly expensive - GPQA cost $100k in 2024, and newer benchmarks can cost over $1 million just for human baseline costs
- Benchmarks often risk being saturated before they're even completed due to the rapid pace of AI development
- Alternative methodologies are explored: uplift studies (real-world impact measurements), expert forecasting/opinion elicitation, and third-party risk assessment
- Anthropic's Opus 4.6 release illustrates the problem - traditional evaluations were maxed out, requiring surveys of researchers to assess capabilities
- The article suggests we're approaching a world where AI development is faster than what we can easily measure
- Companies like Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google DeepMind rely on upper bounds to justify deploying their models, but this approach may soon become untenable
- The fundamental problem is that as AI capabilities improve, benchmarks to measure them become ever more expensive and time-consuming to create
- The author questions what we should do when we live in a world where AI development outpaces our ability to measure it effectively
