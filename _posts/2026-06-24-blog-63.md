## Key Points

- Tom Di Mino, a self-taught AI engineer and amateur linguist, claims to have deciphered the Bronze-age Minoan writing system Linear A that has puzzled experts for over a century.
- Di Mino believes Linear A maps to an extinct Semitic language that was a precursor to Biblical Hebrew, Arabic, and Aramaic.
- His claims are being reviewed by linguistics experts at Rutgers and Cambridge universities.
- Di Mino used Claude Code to build Python scripts that query and cross-reference digitized Linear A databases, enabling systematic hypothesis testing at scale.
- His breakthrough came on May 22, 2024, when he identified the verb root 'nawaya' (meaning 'to dwell') in prayer inscriptions, linking it to similar Hebrew prayers.
- Di Mino's research includes proposed readings for 40 signs (including 13 previously unknown), a lexicon of 408 Linear A terms, and a 9-page draft manuscript.
- The decipherment could revolutionize linguistics similar to the 1952 decipherment of Linear B, which made front page news in the New York Times.
- Unlike previous attempts to prove Linear A was Semitic, Di Mino's solution appears to unlock translations more successfully.
- He has studied classical history, linguistics, and 8 languages since age 18, including Attic Greek, classical Latin, and Sanskrit.
- If validated, this would represent a major breakthrough in understanding Minoan civilization and language history.
