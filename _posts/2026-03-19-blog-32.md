## Key Points

The author asked his AI agent to evaluate its own memory system and make improvements - the agent redesigned its memory, ran self-evaluation, and improved recall from 60% to 93% in just 45 minutes and $2 in API costs.
The memory system originally consisted of markdown files indexed into a SQLite database with Gemini embeddings, with 18,000 chunks across 604 files covering 6,578 session transcripts.
The baseline evaluation showed the system was great at remembering technical events (100% recall), cross-references (100%), and temporal events (100%), but terrible at people context (33%) and decision rationale (25%).
The key problem was that the system captured "what" happened but not "why" - it logged events without recording the reasoning behind decisions, similar to institutional knowledge loss in human organizations.
The AI agent proposed four improvements: add "why" fields to decision logs, compress daily logs to weekly summaries, create searchable people files, and backfill decision rationale from session transcripts.
After implementation, decision rationale recall improved from 25% to 100%, and overall clean recall went from 60% to 93%.
The author discovered that by treating AI as a participant in its own cognitive architecture rather than just a tool to configure, the system could identify and fix its own blindspots.
The experiment suggests asking AI systems about their preferences for memory structure can surface latent patterns and lead to significant improvements without model upgrades or major infrastructure changes.
When the author asked ChatGPT about its memory preferences, it suggested moving from treating conversations as a notebook to treating them as "a changing map."
The author emphasizes that the continuity of AI memory exists in the files rather than in the AI's "consciousness" - the important thing is asking systems how they want to remember and giving them agency to improve their own design.
