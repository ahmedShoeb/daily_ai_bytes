## Key Points

The article explores whether AI application companies should invest in post-training their own models rather than relying on frontier API calls from companies like OpenAI and Anthropic
For application companies, post-training typically means supervised fine-tuning or RL on strong open-weights base models, not pre-training from scratch
The main arguments FOR post-training include: 1) Better unit economics and latency at scale, 2) Differentiation through proprietary data and traces, 3) Building specialized models for pipeline parts that frontier labs don't prioritize
Examples of companies doing post-training include Cursor (Composer 2 on Kimi K2.5), Intercom (Fin Apex 1.0), Cognition (SWE-1.5), Sierra, Decagon, and OpenEvidence
The biggest argument AGAINST post-training is that models may become obsolete quickly as frontier labs release new base models faster than ever - GPT-5 went through multiple versions in months
Post-training talent is scarce and expensive, creating opportunity costs that could be deployed elsewhere in the company
Companies should consider post-training when they have enough proprietary traces to make specialized models meaningfully better than frontier models for specific pipeline parts
New infrastructure like Tinker from Thinking Machine Labs, Prime Intellect's Lab, and Applied Compute has lowered the barrier to entry for post-training
A key principle remains: "no GPUs before PMF" - don't invest in training before achieving product-market fit
For most early-stage application companies, the recommendation is to collect data and traces first, then start with small specialized models for boring pipeline parts before attempting to replace frontier reasoning models
