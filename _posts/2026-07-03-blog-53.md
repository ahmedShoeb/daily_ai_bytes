## Key Points

1. OpenAI has introduced GeneBench-Pro, a research-level benchmark that measures how AI agents handle ambiguity and make consequential judgments in computational biology.
2. The benchmark tests whether models can handle judgment-heavy analysis that real-world computational biology requires, covering genomics, quantitative biology, and translational medicine.
3. GeneBench-Pro specifically measures 'research taste' - the chains of judgment calls that shape an analysis, including handling ambiguity, revising assumptions, and choosing correct analysis paths.
4. It contains 129 challenging questions where models must explore messy datasets, choose appropriate analytical approaches, engage in iterative experimentation, and provide final answers.
5. Problems are built synthetically to avoid benchmark failures, with full causal structure and simulated data-generating processes to ensure correct analytical pathways are required.
6. External domain experts reviewed the problems and found they would be challenging for graduate students, requiring thoughtful analysis with awareness of potential pitfalls.
7. OpenAI's strongest model, GPT-5.6 Sol, achieves a 28.7% pass rate (31.5% with Pro mode), a significant improvement from GPT-5 which scored below 5% on earlier versions.
8. Reviewers estimate each GeneBench-Pro problem would take human experts 20-40 hours to complete at thousands of dollars, while AI inference costs just several dollars per problem.
9. The benchmark reveals that open-source models are more specialized for coding than broader reasoning ability, with GPT models showing stronger performance on high-level scientific reasoning.
10. Despite progress, frontier models still solve fewer than a third of these problems, showing substantial room for improvement in handling ambiguous scientific analysis.
