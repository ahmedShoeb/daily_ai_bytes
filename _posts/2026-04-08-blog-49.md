## Key Points

Microsoft's updated Copilot terms of use (from October) state the AI is 'for entertainment purposes only' and explicitly warns users: 'Use Copilot at your own risk'
The terms note that Copilot 'can make mistakes, and it may not work as intended' and advise against relying on it for important decisions
This legal language contrasts sharply with Microsoft's aggressive marketing of Copilot as a core feature in Copilot+ PCs and deeply integrated into Windows 11 and productivity apps
Microsoft isn't alone - other AI companies like Elon Musk's xAI include similar warnings about AI hallucinations, offensive content, and inaccurate outputs
The warnings highlight a broader industry tension: AI is marketed as essential technology but legally described as unreliable
Real-world incidents like Amazon AWS outages have occurred where engineers allowed AI coding bots to make changes without proper oversight
The disclaimers reflect the gap between AI promise and operational risk, with responsibility for errors ultimately falling on human users and organizations
Human 'automation bias' - trusting machine results over contradictory evidence - becomes more dangerous with AI systems that produce persuasive but flawed work
These legal terms are written by lawyers, not marketing teams, offering a more sober view of AI's current limitations
The fine print suggests that while billions are invested in AI infrastructure, the technology is not yet fully trustworthy
