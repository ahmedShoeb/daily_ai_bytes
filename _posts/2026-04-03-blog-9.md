## Key Points

AI seed startups are now raising $10 million seed rounds at $40-45 million post-money valuations, compared to just $5 million at $25 million valuations in 2024
Investors show little interest in non-AI companies, creating intense competition for AI startups that results in pricing rounds 'years ahead of traction'
Large venture firms are moving into earlier rounds, driving up prices while smaller VC firms often get priced out of deals, leading to fewer seed deals but higher valuations
Companies like Cursor, which hit $100 million in revenue in 12 months, have raised the bar for how fast AI startups can gain traction
VCs defend high valuations by pointing to faster traction - companies now generate revenue immediately with enterprise customers, unlike traditional seed-stage companies
Founder background plays a key role - second-time founders and those with AI research experience command premium valuations, with extreme examples like ex-OpenAI talent raising at $12 billion valuations
Pre-seed is becoming the new seed as investors move earlier to access promising startups before valuations skyrocket
Check sizes have doubled or tripled - some firms now write $4-5 million pre-seed checks compared to $1-2 million just years ago
Higher valuations mean less margin for error - startups have 18 months to hit milestones, with less tolerance for pivots and more scrutiny if progress lags
Founders risk getting 'stuck in between' - too expensive for new investors but without sufficient traction to justify the next round
