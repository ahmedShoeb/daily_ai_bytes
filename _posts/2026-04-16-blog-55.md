## Key Points

- LLM inference is often nondeterministic even at temperature 0, where the model should always choose the highest probability token (greedy sampling)
- The common hypothesis about 'concurrency + floating point non-associativity' explains the numeric differences but misses the root cause of nondeterminism in production systems
- The real culprit is lack of 'batch invariance' - kernel results depend on batch size, and since server load (batch size) varies nondeterministically, inference becomes nondeterministic
- Most kernels (RMSNorm, Matmul, Attention) are theoretically run-to-run deterministic but not batch-invariant, meaning results change when the batch size changes
- To achieve deterministic LLM inference, all operations must be batch-invariant, requiring specialized kernel implementations with consistent reduction strategies
- The authors demonstrate batch-invariant implementations and show 1000 identical completions vs 80 unique completions without their modifications
- Performance impact exists but is manageable - unoptimized implementation increases inference time from 26 to 55 seconds for 1000 sequences
- Deterministic inference enables 'True on-policy RL' where sampling and training produce bitwise identical results, removing the need for off-policy correction
