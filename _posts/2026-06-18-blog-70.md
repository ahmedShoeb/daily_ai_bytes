## Key Points

- The common claim that AI inference GPUs only last 'three years at most' under high utilization comes from an anonymous Twitter source citing an anonymous Google architect
- The source of this quote appears to be a Tegus interview where interviewees are paid to sound confident, creating potential bias in their estimates
- Evidence suggests GPUs can last much longer: Google runs 8-year-old TPUs at 100% utilization, AWS claims never retiring A100 servers, and academic GPU clusters show 6-year lifespans with low failure rates
- Supercomputer data from Oak Ridge's Summit (2018-2024) and Titan (2012-2019) shows GPU survival rates above 90% after 3 years and above 60% after 6 years in properly cooled conditions
- While economic lifespans may be shorter due to newer, more efficient hardware, this doesn't support the 'inference will become too expensive' argument
- Even if newer GPUs are available, cash-poor AI providers can continue profitably serving inference with older hardware if it's still profitable
- The 'three years at most' claim appears popular among AI skeptics but lacks strong evidence compared to data from actual GPU cluster operations
- In an AI winter scenario, AI datacenters could likely run GPUs profitably for 6+ years due to lower infrastructure costs compared to building new datacenters
