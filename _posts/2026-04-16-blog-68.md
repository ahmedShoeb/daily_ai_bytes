## Key Points

- The research paper addresses the challenge of large language models struggling to memorize factual knowledge, which often leads to hallucinations and poor performance on knowledge-intensive tasks.
- The authors formalize fact memorization from an information-theoretic perspective and study how training data distributions affect fact accuracy.
- They demonstrate that fact accuracy becomes suboptimal when the information in training data facts exceeds model capacity, which is exacerbated by skewed fact frequency distributions (like power laws).
- The paper proposes novel data selection methods based solely on training loss that aim to limit the number of facts in training data and flatten their frequency distribution.
- On semi-synthetic datasets with high-entropy facts, their selection method effectively boosts fact accuracy to the capacity limit of models.
- In experiments with language models trained from scratch on an annotated Wikipedia corpus, their method enabled a GPT2-Small model (110M parameters) to memorize 1.3X more entity facts compared to standard training.
- The smaller model with data selection achieved performance matching that of a 10X larger model (1.3B parameters) trained on the full dataset.
- The research was accepted at the Workshop on Navigating and Addressing Data Problems for Foundation Models at ICLR 2026.
- The work addresses a significant scaling problem in LLM training where simply adding more parameters doesn't always improve fact memorization.
