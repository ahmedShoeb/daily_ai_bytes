## Key Points

- American AI was financed on the assumption that frontier models would create a monopoly business with trillion-dollar valuations, but this assumption is breaking as open-weight models commoditize the capability
- Open-weight models from Chinese labs like DeepSeek are closing the performance gap with US closed-frontier models to 6-12 months while offering inference at single-digit cents on the dollar
- US frontier labs and hyperscalers have committed around a trillion dollars to AI capex expecting monopoly-grade returns, creating structural pressure when technology no longer provides that moat
- The open ecosystem (vLLM, llama.cpp, Ollama, LangChain, LlamaIndex) enables wide distribution of open weights, undermining the lock-in assumptions that closed-lab pricing models depend on
- When technology fails to provide natural moats, American capitalism typically manufactures scarcity through regulatory enclosure, vertical integration, or bundled distribution
- Three predictions: Regulatory enclosure targeting Chinese-origin models as security risks, labs moving up the stack to become operators instead of tool vendors, and market split between protected US market and global open ecosystem
- Historical parallel to US auto industry shows protectionist walls lead to protected margins domestically but loss of global competitiveness - from 80% market share in 1980 to below 40% today
- Costs fall on US consumers, small developers, startups, labs' long-term competitiveness, and US global AI influence while narrow beneficiaries include labs, cloud providers, and investors
- Recommendations: Build on open commons, design for jurisdictional flexibility, and account for policy changes in architecture
- The central conflict is between a capital structure requiring manufactured scarcity and an open commons enabling abundance - with US policy choices determining whether AI follows the internet's open success or auto industry's protected decline
